How care is delivered in rural vs urban Ireland — and why it matters

The postcode you live in shapes the care you receive. Here's what that actually looks like on the ground.

Ireland is a country of two realities living side by side. Nearly two-thirds of the population live in urban areas. Rural Ireland covers the vast majority of the country's landmass — with fewer people, fewer services, and a growing gap between where people live and where care is delivered.

Urban Ireland has 100 GPs per 100,000 people. Rural Ireland has 60. 500 rural GPs work alone; many are close to retirement. When they go, there is often no one to replace them. The ICGP estimates Ireland will need 1,200 more GPs by 2040 just to meet growing demand.

Almost 42% of Ireland's family carers live in rural areas. Those providing intensive care in remote locations face a significantly higher risk of mental health difficulties, social isolation, and burnout. In significant parts of Ireland, the drive to a full emergency hospital is 90 minutes or more. Rural patients with chronic conditions face higher mortality rates than their urban counterparts.

The gap between rural and urban care in Ireland is not closing. It is widening. The GP shortage is structural. The distance to hospitals is not going away. And the family carer — already carrying more than their share — is being asked to carry more every year.
