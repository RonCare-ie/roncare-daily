Ireland's Invisible Workforce

One in three women aged 50–69 in Ireland is in the sandwich generation. The work is invisible. The consequences are not.

There is a generation of people who drop the kids to school, sit with an ageing parent at a GP appointment in the afternoon, answer work emails in between, and cook for two households by evening. They are the sandwich generation — adults simultaneously caring for dependent children and ageing parents — and in Ireland, their numbers are growing.

Research from TILDA found that over 79% of sandwich generation women financially supporting their parents were simultaneously financially supporting their children. 71% were also providing non-financial care to parents. One third were caring for grandchildren on top of that. Ireland's old-age dependency ratio is projected to reach 50 by 2057 — 50 people aged 65 or over for every 100 working-age adults.

The work is largely invisible: personal care, emotional support, transport, cooking, household management, GP visits, school runs. It is consistently associated with poorer self-reported health and higher rates of depression. Many in this cohort reduce working hours or move to lower-paid roles, compounding the financial strain and reducing long-term pension contributions.

Ireland's care infrastructure was not built for this demographic reality. The conversation around carer recognition is gaining momentum — but the policy response has not yet caught up. The sandwich generation is not a niche social issue. It is a public health challenge, a workforce issue, and an economic one.
