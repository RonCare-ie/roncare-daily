Today's Briefing

AI-Powered Wearables and Clinical-Grade Cardiac Monitoring Enter Mainstream Healthcare Delivery

University of Chicago researchers demonstrate 99.6% accuracy cardiac patch that processes data directly on the device, advancing point-of-care AI technology for real-time patient monitoring.

Something is shifting in how healthcare administration works — and it's happening fast. Artificial intelligence is moving out of research labs and into the day-to-day systems that keep hospitals and clinics running: appointment booking, patient assignment, prior authorisations, and care coordination. This matters to family carers because when the admin side works better, patients wait less, staff burn out less, and the right care reaches the right person sooner.

The biggest institutional move this week came from the US Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), which created a new Office of Health Technology Products to oversee AI, interoperability, and digital health tools across Medicare and Medicaid. The office includes eight internal groups, with specific teams focused on health information exchange and coordinating data standards between providers, payers, and patients.

CMS also launched its ACCESS model this month — a voluntary ten-year programme where organisations are paid based on patient health outcomes, like lower blood pressure or reduced pain, rather than just the number of services delivered. Care coordination and FDA-cleared digital tools are at the core of this approach.

On hospital floors, AI is already tackling one of the biggest bottlenecks: figuring out which doctor gets which patient. Banner Health, one of the largest non-profit health systems in the US, partnered with Qventus — an AI platform for hospital operations — and reported that the system freed up 369 hours of operating room block time every month by predicting cancellations. A peer-reviewed study published in Frontiers in Medicine found that AI-driven patient redistribution lightened cognitive load on staff and improved how complex cases were shared among doctors.

Physicians have also reported meaningful reductions in time spent writing clinical notes after adopting AI documentation tools. These small changes add up to real breathing room in a packed day.

At the HIMSS AI in Healthcare Forum, experts gathered to work through governance challenges. Their central concern: AI in healthcare is only as good as the data feeding it, and right now that data is often fragmented and inconsistently labelled. Spain's National Health System highlighted its collaborative AI development approach as a possible model for the European Union as it builds a shared European Health Data Space.

What all of this points to is a healthcare system genuinely trying to fix its back office — not just its operating theatres. When AI handles the scheduling, paperwork, and coordination gaps, healthcare staff get more time for actual care, and patients spend less time lost in the system. The hard work now is making sure the governance, data quality, and human oversight keep pace with the technology.
