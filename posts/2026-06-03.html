Ireland's Invisible Workforce

One in three women aged 50–69 in Ireland is in the sandwich generation. The work is invisible. The consequences are not.

There is a generation of people who drop the kids to school, sit with an ageing parent at a GP appointment in the afternoon, answer work emails in between, and cook for two households by evening. It might sound familiar. They are the sandwich generation — adults simultaneously caring for dependent children and ageing parents — and in Ireland, their numbers are growing.

Taking a look at the data

One in three women aged 50–69 in Ireland is currently in the sandwich generation. The majority are providing care to both elderly parents and dependent children at the same time.

This is the result of two converging demographic shifts: people are living longer, and women are having children later. In 2022, over 806,000 people in Ireland were aged 65 or over — more than 17% of the population — a figure projected to double by 2051. Meanwhile the average age of first-time mothers continues to rise. The result is a growing cohort of middle-aged adults caught between two generations of need.

Ireland's old-age dependency ratio is projected to reach 50 by 2057. That means for every 100 working-age adults, there will be 50 people aged 65 or over.

What this actually looks like

The work is largely invisible. It includes personal care, emotional support, transport, cooking, household management, GP visits, school runs, financial transfers, and everything in between.

Research from TILDA — the Irish Longitudinal Study on Ageing at Trinity College Dublin — found that over 79% of sandwich generation women financially supporting their parents were simultaneously financially supporting their children. 71% were also providing non-financial care to parents. One third were caring for grandchildren on top of that.

This is a structural reality with measurable consequences. Informal caregiving of this kind is consistently associated with poorer self-reported health and higher rates of depression. The more hours given, and the more years spent caregiving, the greater the impact.

Moreover, many in this cohort reduce working hours or move to lower-paid, more flexible roles — compounding the financial strain and reducing long-term pension contributions. This is a significant dependency loop that is often overlooked in people who carry out caring roles.

Keeping Up

Ireland's care infrastructure was not built for this demographic reality. Home care and the Fair Deal scheme involve long waiting lists and complex application processes. Carers' allowance remains modest and difficult to access. Unpaid caregiving is not recognised in the pension system. Employer-provided childcare and eldercare support remains inconsistent.

The awareness is definitely growing around carers' wellbeing and rights — but the policy response has not yet caught up.

What's next?

The sandwich generation is not a niche social issue but a public health challenge, a workforce issue, and an economic one. Supporting these carers is not just about easing individual strain — we should be focused on building a sustainable care system for an ageing country.

Digital tools that reduce the administrative and coordination burden of home care — helping families manage care plans, communicate across care teams, and avoid burnout — are one part of that picture. RonCare is a small step toward making the invisible work of home care more manageable, and the people doing it less alone.

The conversation around carer recognition in Ireland is gaining momentum. I look forward to opening up the conversation further.
