How care is delivered in rural vs urban Ireland — and why it matters

The postcode you live in shapes the care you receive. Here's what that actually looks like on the ground.

Understanding the Geography

Ireland is a country of two realities living side by side.

Nearly two-thirds of Ireland's 5.15 million people live in urban areas. Dublin alone is home to over 1.4 million residents. Urban centres — particularly the commuter belts around Dublin, Cork, and Galway — have grown rapidly, with infrastructure, services, and healthcare expanding alongside them.1

Rural Ireland tells a different story. While home to fewer people overall, rural communities cover the vast majority of the country's landmass. Some remote areas have seen population decline and stagnation. The people who remain are older, on average. The services available to them are fewer.2 And the distance between where they live and where care is delivered is growing — not shrinking.

This is the Ireland that family carers live in.

GPs

Urban Ireland has 100 GPs per 100,000 people. Rural Ireland has 60. That's a 40% gap — and it's getting worse.3

500 rural GPs work alone. Many are close to retirement. When they go, there is often no one to replace them. The ICGP estimates Ireland will need 1,200 more GPs by 2040 just to meet growing demand.3

In parts of east Mayo, east Clare, Inishowen in Donegal, and areas of Wexford and Westmeath, a dedicated GP service disappearing entirely is not a worst-case scenario. It is a projection.3

Nurses, Carers and Public Health Nurses

When the GP is unavailable, the Public Health Nurse becomes the primary professional link for an elderly person at home. But research on PHNs working in rural Ireland found they spend significant time on travel due to poor road networks, work in isolation with limited peer support, and face consistent challenges with broadband and mobile connectivity. They are stretched — geographically and professionally.5

Home carers face the same pressures. High turnover means the person who arrives at the door may be meeting the client for the first time, with no handover and no shared record of what happened yesterday.

Almost 42% of Ireland's family carers live in rural areas. Those providing intensive care in remote locations face a significantly higher risk of mental health difficulties, social isolation, and burnout. They are the invisible infrastructure of Irish healthcare.4

Hospitals — Distance and Access

Urban residents benefit from proximity to hospitals and specialists. Rural communities depend on GPs and smaller clinics — and when those are under pressure, the gap widens fast.2

In significant parts of Ireland, the drive to a full emergency hospital is 90 minutes or more. A GP surgery or pharmacy can be 30 minutes away on a good day. Mental health services and emergency response times remain areas of particular concern, where geographic coverage areas stretch capacity to its limits.4

Rural patients with chronic conditions — heart disease, diabetes, respiratory illness — face higher mortality rates than their urban counterparts. Distance is not the only reason. But it is a significant one.7

What Happens in an Emergency?

When something goes wrong at home — a fall, a sudden deterioration, a medication error — the person most likely to respond is a family member, often one who is not medically trained and does not have access to the information they need.

Who knows the medication list? Who has the GP's name? Who knows about the allergy, the mobility issue, the fact that she didn't eat yesterday?

Right now, that information lives in a notebook — if it exists at all.

Conclusion

The gap between rural and urban care in Ireland is not closing. It is widening.

The GP shortage is structural. The distance to hospitals is not going away. The public health nurse cannot be everywhere. And the family carer — already carrying more than their share — is being asked to carry more every year.

Technology will not solve this alone. But a tool that gives the whole care team — wherever they are — one shared space to coordinate, communicate, and stay informed could make a real difference to the people holding this system together.
