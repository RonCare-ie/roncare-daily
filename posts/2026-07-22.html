Today's Briefing

Re:State Report Urgently Calls for Place-Based Strategy to Fix England's Adult Social Care Workforce Crisis

A new analysis warns that chronic recruitment and retention problems in UK care cannot wait for wider funding reform.

Something significant is happening across Europe, the UK, and Ireland right now — and it affects almost every family. The EU is at a demographic turning point: according to Eurostat, the bloc's population will peak around 2026 before beginning a gradual decline, driven largely by an ageing society. By 2050, three in ten people in the EU will be over 65, up from two in ten today, and the share of those aged 85 and over will more than double. These aren't distant projections — health services, care homes, and home support systems are already feeling the strain.

Northern Ireland is right at the sharpest edge of this shift. Recent reports have consistently described it as 'the fastest ageing part of the UK,' and the numbers back that up — the over-65 population is projected to grow by 44.7% over the next 25 years, while those aged 85 and over will more than double, rising from 42,900 to 96,900. Waiting lists are already spiralling, nursing shortages are deep, and cuts to community support groups are leaving older people with fewer places to turn.

In England, the adult social care workforce is under mounting pressure. A report published by the think tank Re:State found that vacancy rates stood at 6.8% in 2024–25 — equivalent to around 111,000 unfilled posts — while staff turnover held at 23.7%. Of care workers looking to leave, 67% say the impact of the job on their health and wellbeing is a key reason, yet only around 16% have ever accessed therapy services. The UK government's planned Fair Pay Agreement aims to improve pay and conditions — but analysis suggests even that would only add around 68p per hour above the National Minimum Wage, leaving a significant gap with equivalent NHS roles.

Ireland is facing its own reckoning. By 2057, one in every three people living in Ireland will be aged 60 or older. Ireland's Minister for Older People Kieran O'Donnell secured a €215 million increase in older persons services funding for 2026, a rise of over 7% on the previous year, including €92 million for the Fair Deal nursing home scheme and 1.7 million additional home support hours. A UCD Ageing Network policy paper warned there is still no statutory home care scheme, and a recruitment and retention crisis in home care services means pay and conditions need significant improvement to make it a sustainable profession.

For families, carers, and the people they look after, these figures tell a human story. More older people need more care — at home, in communities, and in residential settings — and the systems meant to provide that care are stretched, underfunded, and struggling to hold onto their staff. Policymakers across Ireland, Northern Ireland, and England are responding with new funding and reform proposals, but the challenges ahead demand more than piecemeal fixes. The decisions made in the next few years will shape the kind of old age millions of people can expect to have.
