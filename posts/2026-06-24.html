Ireland to Need 50,000 Long-Term Care Beds by 2040 as One in Five Population Ages Over 65

Economic and Social Research Institute projects 60% increase in residential care capacity needed within 15 years to meet demographic shift.

Across Europe, the UK, and Ireland, an ageing population is no longer a distant warning — it's arriving right now. Eurostat projects that by 2050, three in ten people across the EU will be over 65, up from two in ten in 2023. Northern Ireland has been described as the fastest ageing part of the UK, with its over-65 population set to grow by 44.7% over the next 25 years and the over-85s set to more than double. At the same time, health services are already stretched thin — and the people feeling it most are the families trying to hold everything together.

In Ireland, a May 2026 report by the Economic and Social Research Institute (ESRI) — funded by the HSE — found that the country will need more than 2,000 additional public health and community nurses by 2040, with average annual workforce growth of between 2% and 3.2% needed just to keep pace with older people's needs. Minister for Health Jennifer Carroll MacNeill acknowledged that 'increasing capacity, both in terms of workforce and infrastructure, is key to address Ireland's long-term demographic challenges.'

Ireland's Department of Health has confirmed that since 2022, more than 1,300 additional training places have been delivered across health and social care disciplines, with 600 more on track for 2026. These numbers show real movement, but they also show how far there is still to go.

In England, the government has moved to head off the crisis with a significant funding push. Up to £3.7 billion of additional funding has been made available for social care authorities in 2025–2026, including an £880 million increase in the Social Care Grant and the largest ever uplift to Carer's Allowance of £2,000. The government has also committed to around £4.6 billion in additional adult social care funding by 2028–2029, while legislating for the first ever Fair Pay Agreement for care workers — a recognition that low pay has long driven people out of the sector.

Northern Ireland tells a starker story. Former head of the Northern Ireland civil service David Sterling warned that without urgent planning, there will be 'an even bigger crisis in public services before the end of the decade.' Spiralling waiting lists, nursing shortages, and difficulties accessing GPs have already taken hold. Professor Ian Shuttleworth of Queen's University Belfast points out that the North is running into this demographic wave on top of an existing funding crisis, and that increased migration may be the only near-term fix for the healthcare workforce gap.

Across the border, the Republic faces a different but equally pressing challenge. The number of workers paying taxes to support each pensioner will fall from five to three within two decades — a shift that will reshape how we fund health and social care.

What all of this means is that families are going to be asked to do more — and for longer. For policymakers, the window to act is narrow. Ireland's Sláintecare plan and England's care reforms offer a road map, but funding, staffing, and political will need to catch up fast. For anyone caring for a parent or loved one right now, this is not just a policy debate — it is daily life, and it deserves far more than a commission report.
