Ireland's 500,000 Family Carers Gain Financial Relief as Carer's Allowance Income Limits Rise Dramatically

Historic increase in support thresholds allows more Irish families to access carer payments while maintaining employment and other income sources.

Right now, somewhere in Ireland, a woman in her early fifties is dropping her teenager to school, rushing home to help her mother get dressed, and still making it to a part-time job by nine o'clock. She's part of what's quietly being called the sandwich generation — people raising children and caring for ageing parents at the same time. It's one of the most common and least talked-about pressures in Irish family life. People aged 50–59 are the most likely group in Ireland to be providing regular care, often while still parenting and working. Most are doing it without a plan, without much support, and without knowing help even exists.

The weight of it is real and measurable. According to Family Carers Ireland, the average family carer provides 38.7 hours of care every week — a full-time job, unpaid, on top of everything else. Nearly a third are doing 43 hours or more. Because so many sandwich generation carers are women — 34% of women in Ireland do most or all of the care for their main recipient, compared to 19% of men — it often falls hardest on people already stretched across multiple roles. That pattern shapes careers, finances, health, and futures.

Ireland's ageing population is only going in one direction. According to the Central Statistics Office (CSO), the number of people aged 65 and over has grown by 37% since 2015, and that figure is projected to double by 2051. For families in the middle right now, that context matters: the pressure isn't easing, and the system needs to catch up. There are currently 5,556 people on the waiting list for home care in Ireland, even after a 12.5% increase in home support hours since 2022.

There is one thing that could genuinely ease some of the pressure, and most sandwich carers don't know about it. Carer's Allowance pays around €270 a week and you can still work up to 18.5 hours and qualify. From July 2026, the income disregard is increasing to €1,000 per week for a single person and €2,000 for a couple — meaning far more people will be eligible than before. There's also the Carer's Support Grant, a €2,000 tax-free payment made automatically every June, and Carer's Leave, which lets you take up to 104 weeks away from work with your job kept open. You can check what you're entitled to at Citizens Information or apply directly through welfare.ie using form CR1.
