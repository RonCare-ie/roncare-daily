State of Caring 2026 Report: Nearly Half of Irish Family Carers Experience Severe Loneliness Amid Cost Pressures

New research reveals Irish carers are cutting back on food and heating while 48% face acute emotional isolation.

Almost half of Ireland's family carers — 48% — have been diagnosed with a mental health condition. Not burnt out, not stressed, not struggling: diagnosed. That's nearly one in two people who quietly took on the care of a parent, a partner, a child with a disability, and somewhere along the way, their own health broke down too. Behind that number are real people — people who still get up every morning, make the appointments, manage the medications, and hold everything together for someone they love.

The figures are hard to sit with. 35% of Irish family carers have depression. 39% have anxiety. Yet 72% have never once accessed respite care — a break that could make a real difference.

The average carer provides 38.7 hours of care per week, a full working week, without pay, often without a day off. When caring is that constant, there's simply no space left to notice — let alone tend to — what's happening inside.

What makes this harder is the silence around it. Caring can feel like something you're just supposed to do, without complaint. Many carers don't see themselves as carers at all — they see themselves as a son, a daughter, a spouse doing what needs to be done. That identity can make it very difficult to say 'I'm not okay' or to ask for help. But 44% of carers experience physical or emotional harm as a direct result of their caring role. That's not a personal failing — that's what happens when people carry too much, alone, for too long.

The mental health toll of caring doesn't end when the caring does. For those who have lost the person they cared for, 20% go on to develop complicated grief, persistent depression, or significant psychiatric symptoms. Depressive symptoms typically peak within three months of bereavement but can take fifteen months or more to return to baseline. The caring may be over, but the weight of it doesn't just lift.

If any of this sounds familiar — if you recognise yourself or someone you know in these numbers — please know that you're not alone, and you don't have to keep going without support. Talking to your GP is a good first step. So is connecting with Family Carers Ireland, who offer free counselling and carer supports. RonCare (roncare.ie) is a care coordination app built specifically for families managing care at home — it won't fix everything, but it can take some of the daily weight off your shoulders.
