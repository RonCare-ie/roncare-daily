Who Cares? What carers in Ireland are actually entitled to — and why so many never see it.

People in Ireland struggle to secure carer benefits due to fragmented information, strict means-testing, and complex PRSI conditions. The cost of getting it wrong falls entirely on the people already carrying the heaviest load.

People in Ireland struggle to secure carer benefits due to fragmented information, strict means-testing, and complex PRSI (Pay Related Social Insurance) contribution conditions. Navigating the system is notoriously challenging — and the cost of getting it wrong falls entirely on the people already carrying the heaviest load.

The scale of it

Nearly 500,000 people in Ireland are family carers. They provide an average of 38.7 hours of unpaid care per week — collectively, around 19 million hours weekly. The annual replacement cost of that care is estimated at €20 billion. Yet fewer than 100,000 people receive Carer's Allowance.

That gap is not an accident. It is the system working exactly as designed.

What you are actually entitled to

Carer's Allowance is a means-tested weekly payment for people caring for someone due to age, disability, or illness. To qualify, you must be providing full-time care (at least 35 hours per week), living with or near the person you care for, and your household income must fall below a set threshold. From July 2026, the weekly income disregard increases to €1,000 for a single person and €2,000 for a couple — a meaningful improvement, though long overdue.

If you qualify, you also receive: a GP visit card, Free Travel, the Household Benefits Package, and the annual Carer's Support Grant (paid each June).

Carer's Benefit is different — it is a PRSI-based payment for people who leave work or reduce their hours to care. You must have at least 156 weeks of PRSI contributions paid since starting work, plus meet one of several annual contribution thresholds — the simplest being 39 contributions in the relevant tax year (which is the second-last complete tax year before your claim). It is available for up to 104 weeks per person cared for, and can be taken in separate blocks. If you are in receipt of Carer's Benefit, you also qualify for a GP visit card.

Carer's Support Grant is a once-yearly payment of €2,000 (increased from €1,850 in June 2025), paid each June. If you are already receiving Carer's Allowance or Carer's Benefit, you receive it automatically. If you don't qualify for either of those payments, you can still apply for the Grant separately, provided you meet the care requirements. It is one of the more widely available supports — but many carers do not know it exists.

The real experience: what people say

On Reddit, one carer described quitting their job in the UK to return to Ireland and care for their terminally ill mother — only to be told the application could take 12 weeks. "How do people live on nothing for 12 weeks?" they asked. "I am petrified of what it means for me financially in the long run if I have to live off savings."

Another carer, after three and a half years as a primary carer, wrote: "After 3.5 years of primary carer for my Ma, I was broke! Then began a 6-month battle with them to get Carer's Allowance."

These are not edge cases. They are the norm.

Why the system fails the people it's meant to support

The means test punishes partnership. Eligibility is assessed on household income, not individual income. This strips carers — the majority of whom are women — of financial independence and assumes a partner will willingly share their earnings.

The 18.5-hour work cap is a poverty trap. Carers are permitted to work or study for no more than 18.5 hours per week outside the home. Exceed this, and you lose the payment. It prevents carers from maintaining their careers, skills, and professional identity — and offers no pathway back.

Administrative burden is its own crisis. Forms are lengthy and invasive. Carers must repeatedly prove a loved one's permanent disability. And government departments do not talk to each other: securing a cash payment does not automatically grant access to healthcare equipment or community services. The burden of connecting these dots falls on the carer.

The rate has not kept pace with inflation. Carer's Allowance is the only social welfare payment where recipients are expected to provide a minimum of 35 hours of work per week — and receive just €16 more than the basic social welfare rate in return. The current maximum rate is €260 per week (under 66s). Had it simply tracked inflation since 2020, it would need to be around €261 — meaning years of increases have only just closed the gap, rather than actually improving carers' real incomes.

A counter-argument worth acknowledging

Some argue the means test exists to direct limited resources to those most in need, and that expanding entitlements without structural reform could strain an already pressured system. There is also a question about what "recognising" care work actually means — whether a payment changes the underlying power dynamics, or simply papers over them.

These are fair questions. But they do not justify a system where a full-time carer can be financially worse off than someone on standard jobseeker's payments, taxed on income other comparable welfare recipients are not taxed on, and asked to fight through months of process during some of the hardest weeks of their life.

What needs to change

Family Carers Ireland has called for Carer's Allowance to be replaced by a Participatory Income — a payment that recognises informal care as a social contribution rather than framing it as an anti-poverty measure. It is a shift in language, but also in logic: from welfare to recognition.

In the meantime, if you are a carer in Ireland, contact Family Carers Ireland (familycarers.ie) before attempting to navigate the system alone. If your application is delayed, you can apply for Supplementary Welfare Allowance at your local Intreo office while you wait — it is deductible from any arrears owed. You are not just a carer. You are holding a significant part of this country's healthcare system together. That deserves more than a 12-week wait and a form that asks you to prove your loved one is still unwell.
