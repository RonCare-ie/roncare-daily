Today's Briefing

Ireland Has Fastest Ageing Population in EU with Three-Fold Increase Expected in Those Aged 80+ Over Next 20 Years

Demographic pressures demand urgent expansion of services for older people as death projections surge by 90% over three decades.

Europe is at a demographic turning point — and the pressure on health and social care systems is arriving faster than many governments planned for. The EU is projected to enter a period of steady population decline from 2026, and by 2050, three in ten people across the bloc will be over 65. For anyone who has a parent, a grandparent, or a loved one who depends on care, this is not a distant problem — it's already here.

In England, the numbers tell a stark story. The number of people aged 65 and over rose from 9.5 million in 2014 to 11 million in 2024, according to the Health Foundation. Total net spending on adult social care hit £28.7 billion in 2024/25. The King's Fund has found that despite more money flowing in, English councils are actually in a worsening financial position — external debt at councils rose by 10% in 2024/25 and useable reserves fell by 4%, with some authorities cutting libraries and waste services to keep care going.

In Ireland, Minister for Older People Kieran O'Donnell secured a Budget 2026 package that includes funding for 26.7 million hours of home support — an increase of 1.7 million hours — and €92 million in extra funding for the Fair Deal nursing home scheme. A minimum of 22% of all new home support hours will be ringfenced specifically for people with dementia. Across the wider EU, a Bruegel think-tank analysis published in June 2026 found that by 2050, working-age populations will shrink in 22 out of 27 member states, with Eastern and Southern countries like Romania and Italy facing the sharpest declines. The EU fertility rate hit a record low of just 1.37 in 2023.

For families, carers, and the people they love, what all of this means is simple: the current system is being patched rather than fixed, and the clock is ticking. Unpaid carers — often daughters, sons, or spouses — are quietly filling a gap that neither governments nor the market have solved. The Budget investments in Ireland show that politicians know the problem is urgent, but real reform that brings stability, fair pay for care workers, and genuine support for families is still years away. The best thing families can do right now is plan early, ask questions about funding, and push their local representatives to treat social care with the same urgency as the NHS.

For families, carers, and the people they love, the path forward starts with being informed and asking the right questions today.
