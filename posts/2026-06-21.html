71% of Irish Family Carers Struggling to Make Ends Meet, State of Caring 2026 Reveals

<cite index="45-3,45-4">Family Carers Ireland has warned that family carers are being pushed deeper into financial hardship and social isolation, with 71% finding it difficult to make ends meet and 48% experiencing severe loneliness.</cite>

Somewhere in Ireland right now, a woman in her early fifties is dropping her youngest child to school, then driving straight to her mother's house to help her get dressed, take her medication, and feel less alone. She won't clock in anywhere. Nobody will pay her. And she'll do it all again tomorrow. She is one of more than 500,000 people in Ireland quietly holding the healthcare system together — and she is not alone in being a woman doing this work.

The numbers are stark. According to Family Carers Ireland's State of Caring 2026 report, 71% of family carers are struggling to make ends meet. Women do most or all of the care for their main care recipient in 34% of cases, compared to just 19% of men. That gap plays out in careers paused, pensions reduced, and social lives quietly abandoned.

Here in Ireland, the toll is showing up in health statistics that deserve real attention. According to the State of Caring 2026 report, 67% of Irish family carers have been diagnosed or treated for a physical health condition. 48% have been diagnosed with a mental health condition — 35% with depression, 39% with anxiety. The average carer provides 38.7 hours of care per week, which is a full-time job with no pay, no sick days, and no real way to switch off. Yet 72% of family carers have never accessed respite care.

What makes this a gender issue is that these consequences land differently depending on whether you're a man or a woman. Women are more likely to reduce their working hours, leave the workforce entirely, or never return to the same career level. This means lower lifetime earnings, lower pension contributions, and greater risk of poverty in later life — all because they did something that needed doing and no one else stepped up. Ireland's population is ageing faster than anywhere else in Europe, with the 65+ group projected to double by 2051. Without honest conversations about who is absorbing that pressure, the same women will keep carrying it alone.
