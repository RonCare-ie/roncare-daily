The Population Ageing Crisis

The growing pressure on health and social care systems as Europe's population ages

Europe is at a demographic turning point — and the pressure on health and social care is already here. The EU's population is projected to peak in 2026 before beginning a long decline, and by 2050, three in every ten people across the EU will be over 65. In Ireland, the over-65 population is expected to surpass one million within four years, while Northern Ireland is already the fastest-ageing part of the UK. These aren't future projections to file away — they are today's waiting lists, today's staff shortages, and today's families scrambling to find care for a parent.

In England, there are currently 111,000 unfilled care posts, with a turnover rate of 23.7%. According to recent reports, 67% of care workers looking to leave say the job's impact on their health and wellbeing is why they want to go. Karolina Gerlich, CEO of The Care Workers' Charity, put it plainly: 'Care workers give everything to a job that asks a great deal of them, physically and emotionally.' The UK government has committed around £4.6 billion in additional adult social care funding by 2028–29, but critics from ADASS warn that councils are already overspending by hundreds of millions just to meet their current legal duties.

In Ireland, the picture is equally urgent. The Oireachtas Committee on Health heard in June from Seán Moynihan, Chief Executive of ALONE, who warned that over-65s will exceed one million in four years, with nursing home demand set to rise sharply. Age Action told the same committee there is an urgent need for a statutory home care scheme — something Ireland still lacks. The HSE delivered 23.8 million home support hours in 2024, up from 17.8 million in 2020. The ESRI projects that long-term residential care beds will need to increase by at least 61% by 2040 just to keep pace with demand. A recruitment and retention crisis in home care means the supply of carers is not growing fast enough to meet that need.

Across Europe, the number of people needing long-term care is projected to grow from 30.8 million in 2019 to 38.1 million by 2050. Bruegel, the Brussels-based economic think tank, has called on governments to increase public spending on long-term care and link reform to broader goals like gender equality — since women make up the vast majority of both the formal care workforce and those who need care. The EU's Gender Equality Strategy references the importance of improving working conditions in what has long been an underpaid, female-dominated sector.

For families and unpaid carers — the people who quietly fill the gaps every single day — the strain you feel is real, it is documented, and it is shared by millions across Europe. Policymakers now have the data showing the scale of the crisis. What's needed next is the political will to act. Whether that means a statutory home care scheme in Ireland or a properly funded workforce plan elsewhere, the window to get ahead of the curve is closing fast.
