Support & Community for Family Carers

Resources, guidance, and solidarity for those caring for a loved one across Europe

Across Europe, the quiet, often invisible work of unpaid family carers has never been more critical — or more strained. In Ireland alone, family carers provide approximately 19 million hours of unpaid care every week, saving the State an estimated €20 billion per year, a figure highlighted by President Connolly at the first-ever National Young Carers Conference in Dublin earlier this year. Wider European data from Eurocarers underscores the scale: 80% of all care across Europe is provided by informal carers — people who give their time, health, and often their financial security to support a loved one with a chronic illness, disability, or age-related need. This week, a major development in the UK has brought the financial pressures on carers into sharp relief: the UK government has launched a large-scale reassessment of over 200,000 Carer's Allowance cases, after it emerged that unclear guidance left tens of thousands of carers unknowingly building up debt — through no fault of their own.

For carers in the UK, this is a moment of genuine, if long-overdue, relief. The UK government confirmed that around 25,000 carers could see their debts reduced, cancelled entirely, or receive refunds where money was already repaid, following years of confusing guidance on fluctuating earnings that was in place from 2015 to 2025. Importantly, carers do not need to take any action — the Department for Work and Pensions will get in touch directly if anything further is needed. Separately, advocacy pressure from carers and disabled people forced the government to remove proposed cuts to Personal Independence Payment (PIP) from the Universal Credit and Personal Independence Payment Bill, protecting benefit entitlements for an estimated 150,000 carers who had been at risk of losing support by the end of the decade. On top of this, Carer's Allowance has risen from April 2026 to £86.45 per week, with the earnings limit increasing to £204 net per week — meaning some unpaid carers can now earn around £10,000 a year and still receive the benefit.

In Ireland, Budget 2026 brought meaningful if incremental improvements for carers. The maximum weekly rate of Carer's Allowance increased by €10 from January 2026, and Domiciliary Care Allowance rose by €20 to €380 per month. From July 2026, the Carer's Allowance income disregard will increase significantly — to €1,000 for a single person and €2,000 for a couple — allowing carers to earn more without losing their payments. Ireland's annual Carer's Support Grant for 2026 stands at €2,000 per person cared for, is tax-free, and will be paid automatically in June to those already receiving Carer's Allowance, Carer's Benefit, or Domiciliary Care Allowance. Those not on these payments can still apply using form CSG1 from April 2026 onwards. Advocacy groups like Family Carers Ireland continue to push for more structural change — including the abolition of the Carer's Allowance means test, a statutory right to respite, and the full rollout of a Home Support Scheme — recognising that many carers still struggle with financial strain, poor services, and long waiting lists.

Mental health and respite remain the most urgent unmet needs for carers on both islands. Research consistently shows that carers who cannot take regular breaks are at growing risk of burnout, isolation, and serious mental ill-health. In Ireland, the HSE provides a range of free supports, including free helplines, peer support groups run by community organisations, and a downloadable Family, Carer and Supporter Guide. Family Carers Ireland offers a confidential Care Line, in-home respite care, counselling, and wellbeing programmes, and Care Alliance Ireland runs an online peer support group moderated by health professionals and fellow carers. In the UK, Carers UK operates a free helpline (0808 808 7777, Monday to Friday, 9am–6pm) and online meetups, while Mind and Rethink Mental Illness both offer dedicated support for carers of people with mental health needs, including helplines and local group finders. Respite care — whether a few hours at a day centre, an overnight arrangement, or a short residential stay — is increasingly recognised not as a luxury but as essential infrastructure: it prevents burnout, restores balance, and ultimately helps carers sustain their role over the long term.

If you are a family carer reading this, know that you are not alone — and that support exists right now, even if it can feel hard to find. One concrete step you can take today: if you are in Ireland and not yet receiving Carer's Allowance, Carer's Benefit, or Domiciliary Care Allowance, you can still apply for the €2,000 Carer's Support Grant for 2026 using form CSG1, available from April 2026 through the Department of Social Protection. If you are in the UK and have ever received Carer's Allowance alongside variable earnings, check whether you may be among the 200,000 cases under government review — you do not need to contact the DWP; they will reach out to you. And wherever you are, reaching out to a carer support organisation — whether Family Carers Ireland, Carers UK, or your local HSE or NHS team — is a sign of strength, not weakness. The care you give matters enormously. So does the care you receive.
