From hospital to home — the shift that has to happen

The way we deliver healthcare is changing. Not as a preference — as a necessity.

Ireland's hospitals are under pressure that will not ease on its own. An ageing population, a nursing workforce stretched beyond capacity, and a chronic disease burden that grows every year. The system, as designed, cannot absorb what's coming. Something has to shift.

Stay Left, Shift Left

Originally introduced by Martin Curley as CIO of Ireland's HSE, and now adopted within the United Nations Global Digital Health community, Stay Left, Shift Left is a framework for reorienting healthcare away from hospitals and towards the person, the community, and the home.

It has two core ideas. Stay Left is about prevention — keeping people well, enabling self-care, and managing chronic conditions before they escalate into crises. Shift Left is about decentralisation — moving patients out of acute settings and back to community or home-based care as quickly and safely as possible.

The goal, in short: the right care, in the right place, at the right time. And for most people, most of the time, that place is not a hospital bed.

What this looks like in practice

This is not a theoretical framework. It is being built right now.

Ireland's Enhanced Community Care Programme — a cornerstone of Sláintecare — is already demonstrating what's possible. In 2024, over 7,000 patients were assessed using mobile X-ray services, with 95% treated at home, avoiding hospital transfer entirely. Across the same period, hospital admissions for chronic disease fell by 15% among those supported by community teams.

Virtual wards — a direct expression of Shift Left — are now live across Ireland. Since launching at St. Vincent's University Hospital and University Hospital Limerick in mid-2024, over 1,900 patients have been admitted, generating more than 18,000 virtual bed days. In Letterkenny, a virtual ward focused on respiratory disease produced an 18% reduction in acute hospital admissions in 2025 alone. Four additional virtual wards have since opened, with more planned.

These are patients who would otherwise be in hospital. They are receiving the same level of clinical monitoring and care — at home.

Ireland's Digital for Care 2030 framework sets the direction for what comes next: a National Shared Care Record, a HSE patient app, remote monitoring tools, and AI-assisted clinical decision-making — all oriented toward keeping care connected and closer to people's lives.

The question this raises

If the plan is for more care to happen at home, the home needs to be ready to receive it.

That means asking harder questions about who is actually in the home — who is monitoring, supporting, coordinating, and absorbing the care burden that used to sit inside a hospital ward. In many cases, the answer is a family member. Often a woman. Often without formal training, financial support, or recognition.

Stay Left, Shift Left is a sound framework. But its success depends on what we build around it — not just digital tools and virtual wards, but the human infrastructure that makes home-based care sustainable. Carers need support, not just patients. That is the next conversation.
